Strategic Report
The directors present their strategic report for the year ended 31 December 2023.
Review of the Business
The company's performance in 2023 reflects solid growth and prudent financial management, with key highlights across both financial and non-financial metrics.
Financial Performance:
Revenue Growth: The company achieved a 3.81% increase in turnover, reaching £226.3 million, up from £218 million in 2022. This steady growth is attributed to effective product availability management, particularly through strategic stockpiling when shortages were anticipated, positively impacting sales performance.
Profitability: Gross profit rose to £18.8 million, an increase of £1.9 million from the previous year, driven by improved purchasing discounts and a well-calibrated pricing and promotional strategy. Operating profit saw an even more pronounced rise, growing by 27.2% to £6.1 million from £4.8 million. This impressive growth is largely due to enhanced gross margins and disciplined cost management, keeping operating expenses below inflation levels.
Cash Reserves: The company’s cash generation has been robust, with cash and cash equivalents increasing to £20.7 million from £19.1 million in 2022. This healthy liquidity positions the company strongly, providing financial flexibility and headroom for future investments and mitigating external economic uncertainties.The company's turnover increased by 3.81% to £226.3 million from £218 million in 2022.  We have continued to improve and manage availability of products during this period and where shortage was expected we carried higher level of stock, which resulted in the favourable impact on sales. 
Non-Financial Performance
The company closely monitors key non-financial indicators such as customer satisfaction, staff turnover, and stakeholder relationships. These metrics are vital in maintaining a positive company culture, strong customer loyalty, and solid relationships with suppliers and other stakeholders. Regular feedback is gathered to continuously improve these areas. 
Principal Risks and Uncertainties
The Board has implemented internal controls to facilitate the identification, assessment and management of risk, as a reasonable assurance against adverse implications. The principal risks determined by the Board, corresponding controls and mitigation actions are set out below and is not in particular order of priority. This represents the company's current profile and is not intended to be an exhaustive list of risks and uncertainties that may arise. 
Regulatory Compliance: The business operates in a highly regulated environment, covering areas such as health and safety, employment law, and data protection. The board is vigilant about regulatory changes, regularly updating policies and ensuring relevant employee training to maintain compliance. Failure to do so could harm profitability and reputation.
Competition: The wholesale industry remains intensely competitive, with pressure from both national retailers and regional independent wholesalers. The company competes effectively through a combination of competitive pricing, strong value propositions, and attractive promotions, ensuring it remains responsive to market shifts.
Post-Brexit Effects: The company capitalized on Brexit by strategically holding higher stock levels, which bolstered sales. However, this benefit is unlikely to recur, and future profit margins may face pressure as the market adjusts to new conditions.
Interest Rate Fluctuations: The company has benefited from the current high interest rates, but a potential reduction, as predicted, could impact profitability. Proactive financial planning will be necessary to mitigate this risk.
Supplier Relationships and Credit Risk: Maintaining strong supplier relationships is critical to ensuring product availability. Any disruption in supply or reduction in supplier credit could adversely affect operations. The company mitigates this by closely managing supplier performance and maintaining diverse sourcing channels.

Principal Risks and Uncertainties - continued
Mitigation Strategies:
Credit Risk Management: The company conducts stringent credit checks on new customers and employs robust credit control procedures. Additionally, it secures guarantees or undertakings from customers to safeguard against defaults.
Operational Resilience: IT systems are essential for business continuity, and the company has invested in comprehensive backup procedures, network security, and independent cybersecurity expertise. This mitigates risks related to system failures and cyber threats.
Health and Safety: The company prioritizes workplace safety, ensuring compliance with regulatory standards to avoid incidents that could result in financial or reputational damage. Policies and procedures are regularly reviewed and enforced across the business.
Future Developments
The company is well-positioned for future growth, with the directors intending to build on the strategies that have driven consistent performance. While inflationary pressures and broader economic uncertainties remain, the company’s strong cash reserves provide a solid foundation for investment in new sales outlets and other growth opportunities.
The management team is aware of the challenges ahead, including competitive pressures and the normalization of post-COVID market dynamics, but remains confident in the resilience of the business model. Strategic investments and continued focus on operational efficiency will be crucial in driving sustainable growth. 
While we remain cautious about the current economic climate, we hope to demonstrate the strength of our business model with the continued support of our employees a successful relationship with our suppliers and customers.

Section 172(1) Statement
The directors are committed to promote the success of the company, and in doing so, complying with their duties under Section 172 of the Companies Act 2006. In doing this, the directors have given careful considerations to the following factors:

- the need to foster the company's relationship; 
- the impact of the company's operations on the community and the environment; 
- the desirability of the company's maintaining a reputation for high standards of business conduct: and

- Employees 
- Shareholders 
- Suppliers, insurers and contractors 
- Customers 
- Local communities and the environment 
- Government and regulatory authorities 
The Board is committed to effective engagement with all of its stakeholders. The relevance of each stakeholder group may differ and, as such, as part of its engagement with stakeholders, the board understands the relative interests and priority of each relevant stakeholder group and have regard to these, as appropriate, in its decision making. The board acknowledges that not every decision it makes will necessarily result in a positive outcome for all stakeholder groups, however, they consider this as part of wider consideration of all stakeholder group for effective decision making. 
Every month, the Board and management team undertakes a review where they discuss the financial and strategic performance of the company and the impact of significant decisions taken in the period. The aim is to identify factors which would lead to any positive or negative impact, financial and non-financial, on the status of the company's stakeholders including its employees, shareholders, suppliers, customers, communities, the environment, regulators and the government. 
The Board and its management team regularly share reports on issues concerning employees, shareholders, customers, suppliers, insurance, contractors, communities, the environment, regulators and the government, which takes into account in its discussion and in its decision- making process under section 172. Also, the Board seeks to understand interests and views of the stakeholders and engage with them directly as appropriate. 
The risk management framework and principal risks and uncertainties are stated as set out above.

3.7. Investment Properties
All investment properties are carried at fair value determined annually and derived from the current market rents and investment property yields for comparable real estate, adjusted if necessary for any difference in the nature, location or condition of the specific asset. No depreciation is provided for. Changes in fair value are recognised in the profit and loss account.
3.8. Investments
Investments in quoted and unquoted shares are initially stated at cost and subsequently at a fair value. Changes in fair value are recognised in profit or loss.
3.9. Leasing and Hire Purchase Contracts
Assets obtained under finance leases are capitalised as tangible fixed assets. Assets acquired under finance leases are depreciated over the shorter of the lease term and their useful lives. Assets acquired under hire purchase contracts are depreciated over their useful lives. Finance leases are those where substantially all of the benefits and risks of ownership are assumed by the company. Obligations under such agreements are included in the creditors net of the finance charge allocated to future periods. The finance element of the rental payment is charged to the profit and loss account so as to produce a constant periodic rate of charge on the net obligation outstanding in each period.

Rentals applicable to operating leases where substantially all of the benefits and risks of ownership remain with the lessor are charged to profit and loss account as incurred.
3.10. Stocks and Work in Progress
Stocks are stated at the lower of cost and estimated selling price less costs to complete and sell. Cost is determined using the weighted average method. Costs of purchased stock are determined after deducting rebates and discounts. A provision is made for obsolete and slow-moving items. 
At each reporting date, stocks are assessed for impairment. If stocks are impaired, the carrying amount is reduced to its selling price less costs to complete and sell; the impairment loss is recognised immediately in profit or loss.

3.12. Financial Instruments
Classification
Financial instruments are classified and accounted for, according to the substance of the contractual arrangement, as either financial assets, financial liabilities or equity instruments. An equity instrument is any contract that evidences a residual interest in the assets of the company after deducting all of its liabilities.
Recognition and measurement
Debt instruments (other than those wholly repayable or receivable within one year), including loans and other accounts receivable and payable, are initially measured at present value of the future cash flows and subsequently at amortised cost using the effective interest method. Debt instruments that are payable or receivable within one year, typically trade payables or receivables, are measured, initially and subsequently, at the undiscounted amount of the cash or other consideration, expected to be paid or received. However if the arrangement of a short-term instrument constitute a financing transaction, like the payment of a trade debt deferred beyond normal business terms or financed at a rate of interest that is not a market rate or in case of an out-right short-term loan not at a market rate, the financial asset or liability is measured, initially at the present value of the future cash flow discounted at a market rate of interest for a similar debt instrument and subsequently at a amortised cost. 
Financial asset that are measured at cost and amortised cost are assessed at the end of each reporting period for objective evidence of impairment. If objective evidence of impairment is found, an impairment loss is recognised in the Statement of Income.
For financial assets measured at amortised cost, the impairment loss is measured as the difference between asset's carrying amount and the present value of estimated cash flows discounted at the asset's original effective interest rate. If a financial asset has a variable interest rate, the discount rate for measuring any impairment loss is current effective interest rate determined under the contract.
For financial assets measured at cost less impairment, the impairment loss is measured as the difference between an asset's carrying amount and best estimate, which is an approximation of the amount that the Company would receive for the asset if it were to be sold at the balance sheet date.
Financial assets and liabilities are offset, and the net amount reported in the Balance Sheet when there is an enforceable right to set off the recognised amounts and there is an intention to settle on a net basis or to realise the asset and settle the liability simultaneously.

3.14. Taxation
Income tax expense represents the sum of the tax currently payable and deferred tax.
The tax currently payable is based on taxable profit for the year. Taxable profit differs from profit as reported in the statement of comprehensive income because of items of income or expense that are taxable or deductible in other years and items that are never taxable or deductible. The company's liability for current tax is calculated using tax rates that have been enacted or substantively enacted by the end of the reporting period.
Deferred tax is recognised on timing differences between the carrying amounts of assets and liabilities in the financial statements and the corresponding tax bases used in the computation of taxable profit. Deferred tax liabilities are generally recognised for all taxable timing differences. Deferred tax assets are generally recognised for all deductible temporary differences to the extent that it is probable that taxable profits will be available against which those deductible timing differences can be utilised. The carrying amount of deferred tax assets is reviewed at the end of each reporting period and reduced to the extent that it is no longer probable that sufficient taxable profits will be available to allow all or part of the asset to be recovered.
Deferred tax assets and liabilities are measured at the tax rates that are expected to apply in the period in which the liability is settled or the asset realised, based on tax rates (and tax laws) that have been enacted or substantively enacted by the end of the reporting period. Deferred tax liabilities are presented within provisions for liabilities and deferred tax assets within debtors. The measurement of deferred tax liabilities and assets reflect the tax consequences that would follow from the manner in which the Company expects, at the end of the reporting period, to recover or settle the carrying amount of its assets and liabilities.
Current or deferred tax for the year is recognised in profit or loss, except when they related to items that are recognised in other comprehensive income or directly in equity, in which case, the current and deferred tax is also recognised in other comprehensive income or directly in equity respectively.
3.15. Provisions and Contingencies
Provisions
Provisions are recognised when the company has a present legal or constructive obligation as a result of past events; it is probable that an outflow of resources will be required to settle the obligation; and the amount of the obligation can be estimated reliably.
Contingencies
Contingent liabilities are not recognised. Contingent liabilities arise as a result of past events when (i) it is not probable that there will be an outflow of resources or that the amount cannot be reliably measured at the reporting date or (ii) when the existence will be confirmed by the occurrence or non-occurrence of uncertain future events not wholly within the company’s control. Contingent liabilities are disclosed in the financial statements unless the probability of an outflow of resources is remote.
Contingent assets are not recognised. Contingent assets are disclosed in the financial statements when an inflow of economic benefits is probable.
